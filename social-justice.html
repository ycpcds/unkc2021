Global Digital Exclusion

UNICEF: Two thirds of the world’s school-age children – or 1.3 billion children aged 3 to 17 years old – do not have internet connection in their homes, according to a new joint report from UNICEF and the International Telecommunication Union (ITU). “That so many children and young people have no internet at home is more than a digital gap –it is a digital canyon,” said Henrietta Fore, UNICEF Executive Director. “Lack of connectivity doesn’t just limit children and young people’s ability to connect online. It prevents them from competing in the modern economy. It isolates them from the world. And in the event of school closures, such as those currently experienced by millions due to COVID-19, it causes them to lose out on education. Put bluntly: Lack of internet access is costing the next generation their futures.”

GIGA

Launched by ITU and UNICEF in 2019, Giga is a global initiative to connect every school to the internet and every young person to information, opportunity and choice. It aims to ensure that every child is equipped with the digital public goods they need, and empowered to shape the future they want. For more information, visit www.gigaconnect.org.

Social Effects of Digital Exclusion

  • Barrier to studies and knowledge.
  • Segregation of wealthy and impoverished peoples, ethnic and racial disparity.
  • Excluded from political activism: includes ability to network with others on a global scale to facilitate challenges of dominating regimes. (Apple 2009).
  • Health systems offer many on-line services.
  • Community involvement.

Source

Studies on Disability and Internet Access

  • Significantly improved frequency and quality of social interaction in people with disabilities (Guo* et al., 2005; Kydland et al., 2012).
  • Associated with lower levels of loneliness (Sum et al., 2008).
  • Significant for social integration and psychological well-being (Berkowsky, 2012).
  • For people with learning disabilities (Löfgren-Mårtenson, 2008): Internet as a way of having a private life away from interference by carers.

Statistics for Inequality in Digital Skills and Access

  1. Men and women: Of those having zero basic digital skills in 2018 61% were women
  2. In 2018 12% of 11-18 year olds reported having no internet access at home from a computer/ tablet, 68% who did have home internet access reported they would find it difficult to complete school work without it.
  3. In 2017 56% of adult internet non- users were disabled, higher than the proportion of disabled adults in the population as a whole (estimated as 22%)
  4. For Internet non-users between 16-24 years, 60% were disabled in 2017.

Source: Office for National Statistics - Internet Users, Labour Force Survey (LFS) 

Though the percentage of disabled adults not using the internet has been declining, in 2018, it was 23.3% compared with only 6.0% of those without a disability

Notes

“Internet non-users” refers to those who have never used the internet or last used it more than 3 months ago.

"Disability" is defined using the Government Statistical Service (GSS) harmonised definition for the Statistical Measures of Disability, as someone who has a current physical or mental health condition(s) or illness(es) lasting or expected to last 12 months or more and that limits their ability to carry out day-to-day activities.

The Tech Partnership Basic Digital Skills Framework 1 

five basic digital skills that can be used to measure digital inclusion and the activities someone should be able to do to demonstrate each skill. These are:

  1. Managing Information: using a search engine to look for information, finding a website visited before or downloading or saving a photo found online.
  2. Communicating: sending a personal message via email or online messaging service or carefully making comments and sharing information online.
  3. Transacting: buying items or services from a website or buying and installing apps on a device.
  4. Problem Solving: verifying sources of information online or solving a problem with a device or digital service using online help.
  5. Creating: completing online application forms including personal details or creating something new from existing online images, music or video.

Research commissioned by Carnegie UK Trust indicates that Digital exclusion and Social exclusion go hand in hand.

Schejter et al, 2015, looks at quantifying how much digital exclusion affects social exclusion. This research was limited by the available data.

A relationship between social inequalities and access to digital technologies was identified by Van Dijk in 2005 and RSE in 2014.

Digital Exclusion: Unequal access and capacity to use information and communication technologies. 

Social Exclusion: where individuals are unable to fully participate in society to their detriment and that of society as a whole.
