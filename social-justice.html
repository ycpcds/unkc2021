Global Digital Exclusion

UNICEF: Two thirds of the world’s school-age children – or 1.3 billion children aged 3 to 17 years old – do not have internet connection in their homes, according to a new joint report from UNICEF and the International Telecommunication Union (ITU). “That so many children and young people have no internet at home is more than a digital gap –it is a digital canyon,” said Henrietta Fore, UNICEF Executive Director. “Lack of connectivity doesn’t just limit children and young people’s ability to connect online. It prevents them from competing in the modern economy. It isolates them from the world. And in the event of school closures, such as those currently experienced by millions due to COVID-19, it causes them to lose out on education. Put bluntly: Lack of internet access is costing the next generation their futures.”

The Tech Partnership Basic Digital Skills Framework 1 

five basic digital skills that can be used to measure digital inclusion and the activities someone should be able to do to demonstrate each skill. These are:

  1. Managing Information: using a search engine to look for information, finding a website visited before or downloading or saving a photo found online.
  2. Communicating: sending a personal message via email or online messaging service or carefully making comments and sharing information online.
  3. Transacting: buying items or services from a website or buying and installing apps on a device.
  4. Problem Solving: verifying sources of information online or solving a problem with a device or digital service using online help.
  5. Creating: completing online application forms including personal details or creating something new from existing online images, music or video.

The 'Life World' of Children

Critical Pedagogy and Inclusion: The Pedagogy underpinning our curriculum includes that of Freire whereby teachers support students to, in Giroux's words: ‘...read both the word and the world’ critically ‘...in order to form practical strategies for inclusion and justice’ 

A 1999 study by Max van Marren argues that we need to have congnisance of the ‘life world’ of children, including the socio-political contexts that influence their opportunities and experiences.

The account of 5th Grade pupils from Cabrini Green, Chicago, one of the most infamous and recognizable housing projects in the U.S.   The programme, Project Citizen set up projects for young people to understand public policy. By tackling an identified problem, students would learn how to effect change in their schools and communities.

Schultz argues that this is an example of students finding relevance: ‘experientially demonstrating their understanding of the data they collected from tangible, real-world experiences that they initiated and care about deeply.’  If YP are more engaged in the curriculum and it is relevant to their lives, attainment, and potentially the attainment gap, will improve. Digital technology will be one of the most significant parts of young people's lives in 15 years. A curriculum for the 2030s needs to be informed by pupils’ interests. The most universally esteemed interests in Young People's lives today are media related.

Internet Skills and Attainment

  • According to Van Dijk and Van Deursen (2011), the development of digital skills is affected by social environments and patterns of learning through family, friends, schooling and workplace.
  • A study from 2014 by Van Deursen et al, looking at patterns of learning digital skills in the Netherlands showed that: Of internet users who were completely self-reliant a large majority were males and those with higher educational attainment, women and those with lower to medium attainment were more prevalent in the groups who needed support with digital skills. 
  • In 2006, a study by Jackson et al. (2006) showed that children from socially deprived backgrounds who use the internet were shown to have higher scores in standardised tests. 
  • The Technology-Enhanced Learning Research Programme, in 2012, stated: to ‘prosper in the 21st Century, people need to be confident digital collaborators and communicators’.
