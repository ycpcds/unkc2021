Our Recommendations


Recommendations for a Critical Digital Literacy Curriculum for Scotland 2035

STAKEHOLDERS AND CURRICULUM DEVELOPMENT

We propose that curriculum content is decided through an ongoing democratic process, representing the views of both education professionals and pupils.

This could look something like the following:

Teachers registered with the GTCS can opt-in to voting on candidates to join a representative panel. Half of these candidates are teachers, and half of these candidates are educationalist academics. There must be explicit and consistent encouragement and support (perhaps including financial support) for candidates from a diverse range of socio-economic and cultural backgrounds to be represented. Panel members will represent each constituent region of Scotland. The proportion of panel members teaching at independent schools cannot exceed the proportion of the Scottish school population enrolled at independent schools. The proportion of panel members teaching at special schools, or specialising in a related academic area, must be at least equal to the proportion of pupils in special schools, units, and/or who require additional support for learning in Scotland. This professional panel would convene each year to propose and vote on amendments to the knowledge-based content of the curriculum, to be implemented in the next academic year. Yearly amendments to the curriculum would require teachers to engage in relevant annual Continued Professional Development (CPD). This will ensure that the knowledge content of the curriculum remains flexible to changes in the development and instrumentalisation of digital technologies.

Each year, the pupils at every Scottish school will be supported by teachers to produce a report on the digital literacy knowledge produced (or co-produced) through learner-centred pedagogy. These pupil reports may include reflections on digital literacy project work, on critical engagement with issues related to digital literacy or on technological developments, on interschool and international knowledge building work through pupil-led online learning spaces, or, on how technology has been used to support learning and inclusion. Online Learning Spaces (OLS) could be central to this process.

Additionally, all pupils in Scottish schools can opt-in to voting for candidates to join a representative panel. There will be at least one panel-member representing each year group, from P1-S6, and panel members will represent each constituent region of Scotland. The proportion of panel members attending independent schools cannot exceed the proportion of the Scottish school population enrolled at independent schools. The proportion of panel members attending special schools, units, and/or who require additional support for learning must be at least equal to the percentage of that demographic in the Scottish school population. Facilitators will be elected by the professional panel to support the pupil panel in aggregate data from the annual pupil reports, and synthesise this information to produce a report on the findings of the learner-centred portion of the curriculum. These reports will be used by the professional council to inform their decisions about amendments to the knowledge-based portion of the curriculum.

KNOWLEDGE-BASED AND LEARNER-CENTERED

We propose that the ideal curriculum for 2035 would include knowledge-based criteria to provide equitable access to the same valuable knowledge for all learners, as far as possible. This knowledge would be considered valuable not due to its’ worth according to technological rationality, but due to a) its’ worth in providing access to living a good life and b) its’ worth in developing the criticality necessary for liberatory activism. This would include specific technological skills, such as coding. It would also include critical analysis of how digital technology is instrumentalised and, in turn, instrumentalises us. This knowledge content would also include both historical and contemporary analysis of the development of technologies, their political effects and functions, and moment of resistance – where digital technologies have been used successfully for liberatory ends – focusing on global, local, and national perspectives. At an early level, this might be as simple as identifying advertisements, and engaging in knowledge-building discussions about the functions and effects of advertisements. At an advanced level, this might be as complex as using historiographical debate to synthesise an informed position on the political implications of the use of data mining by Cambridge Analytica.

As discussed above, we also propose that a learner-centered pedagogical approach be used, in part, to inform ongoing amendments to knowledge content of the curriculum. This would be achieved through pupil-led online learning spaces. See Here for further discussion.

DISCIPLINARY AND INTERDISCIPLINARY

We propose that both disciplinary and interdisciplinary approaches be included in the ideal curriculum for 2035. We acknowledge the epistemic value of historically-produced subject-specific knowledge, while at the same time valuing the possibility of new and alternative knowledge production through interdisciplinary learning. The knowledge-content prescribed in our curriculum would feature both epistemological systems. For example, option subjects such as Computing Science, Digital Media, Technology History, or Digital Politics and Philosophy would adhere to specific disciplinary and interdisciplinary knowledge content. Additionally, the development of specified Digital Critical Literacy knowledge outcomes, for all pupils as far as possible, would be the responsibility of all practitioners on an interdisciplinary basis.

DECOLONISATION OF THE CURRICULUM

We propose that decolonisation theory should inform all areas of the curriculum. We disagree with the view that digital technology has less to do with politics than other areas of the curriculum.

A commitment to decolonisation of the curriculum would involve specific knowledge content; for example, the use of global perspectives and intersectional analysis of oppressive systems (including class, race, and imperialism among other factors) in regards to historical and contemporary analysis of the political function of digital technologies.

It would require that a diversity of perspectives is proactively sought in design of the curriculum and its annual amendments. It would also require that pupils have an opportunity to co-produce alternative forms of knowledge and power through OLS – or elsewhere – and that this knowledge is incorporated into the curriculum through the annual review.

It might involve a professional commitment to an ethical framework that includes the aims of decolonisation, through the GTCS’s SPR. Specific practices for decolonisation would not be mandated, to allow for some plurality of professional perspectives and approaches.

Further, our curriculum would mandate a critical pedagogical approach that restructures the power dynamic between teacher and learner, therefore allowing for democratic dialogue and the production and co-production of non-hegemonic knowledge (and, therefore, non-hegemonic power). Teachers would need to be supported through Initial Teacher Education (ITE) and Continued Professional Development (CPD) to develop an informed position and critical engagement with the politics of education and pedagogy.

ASSESSMENT

We propose that all assessments be formative. Forms of assessment traditionally associated with summative assessment (e.g. tests) will be used formatively to progress learning or to identify teaching and learning needs. Summative assessment may be used as evidence for professional investigations into the relative efficacy of specific approaches to teaching – efficacy in progressing learning, or in intervening in the social reproduction of inequality (e.g. lowering the poverty-based attainment gap, or other measures of wealth inequality). Teachers will share their summative data and findings from these investigations at a local and national level. This will require that teachers are given significant support through ITE and CPD to implement a holistic Assessment if for Learning (AfL) approach supported by a deep understanding of the politics of assessment and of a psychodynamic understanding of the relationship between attachment, feedback, and learning. It will also require a professional commitment to the use of AfL approaches to intervene in the social reproduction of inequality through the GTCS’s SPR.
