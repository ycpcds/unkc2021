What is political about ICT?

In 1985, Donna Haraway’s essay A Cyborg Manifesto envisioned a world where a ‘posthuman’ breakdown between the human-machine (or nature-culture) binary could allow for political liberation. In his 1996 essay, A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace, John Perry Barlow positioned the internet as a stateless space, with the potential to facilitate alternative power through the coproduction of knowledge. Does ICT fulfil this potential? To this day, many see the internet as a force of democratic good – allowing for greater communication efficiency, security, and equal access to information across borders. Others, however, are critical of the idea that we know more and have more power due to the internet and digital technologies. Some key ideas aligned with this criticism are:

  • (Super)complexity: The information provided through ICT is too much for us to understand. In 2000, Ronal Barnett argues that too much information and too many epistemological and ideological systems have led to a collapse of knowledge.
  • Apophenia: In 2016, Hito Steyerl argued that the misrecognition of patterns in data-analysis by AI has led to a collapse of knowledge.
  • Societies of Control: This expands on the Foucauldian idea of societies of discipline. In 1990, Gilles Deleuze argued that the coercive control of society has moved beyond the realm of discipling spaces – the school, the prison, the hospital – to continuous surveillance and measuring of populations.
  • The Implosion of the Social: In 1985, Jean Baudrillard argued that mass media has replaced older social power dynamics. Surveillance, statistical modeling, and predictive analytics, rather than measuring something real about society, function instead to make something real about society.
  • Hyperconnectivity: In 2017, Salvatore Iacones uses Foucauldian biopolitics to argue that the ‘hyperconnectivity’ produced through the network of bodies and information in ICT represents a new political form of ‘life’. Our lives are now inextricable from ICT. This has led to an intensification of alienation.
  • The Collapse of Public/Private: In 2013, Jonathan Crary argued that the internet has led to the collapse of any remaining division between the ‘private’ life of the individual and labour. We now work (at our jobs or to produce data) at all hours of the day through the omnipresence of the internet.
  • Globalisation [link to Crary, J., 2013, and Scholz, T., 2012; 2017 in bibliography]: Many have argued that the internet is instrumental in the acceleration of globalised social relations according to the needs of neoliberal economic systems.

These criticisms may be helpful in understanding the politics behind the following aspects of digital culture:

  • Fake news
  • Conspiracy Theories
  • Ideological radicalisation and polemical discourse (from critiques of ‘cancel culture’ to terrorism)
  • Data mining and predictive analytics in geopolitics and war.
  • Advertising (using data-mining and predictive analytics)
  • Propaganda and statecraft (including advertising AI using predictive analytics and ‘bots’)
  • Surveillance (state, corporate, peer and self-surveillance)
  • Social alienation and mental health epidemics. (This may relate to the rise in depression, anxiety, eating disorders, porn addiction, and other illnesses.
  • Crime and Violence (black-market trading on the dark web, human trafficking, modern slavery, county lines, grooming and child sexual abuse, stalking, harassment, bullying, revenge porn, doxxing, swatting, hacking, extorsion and blackmail)
